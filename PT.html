What We Built

What did we build?
We built The Kesararam With Digital!
A bilingual driving theory education platform for Cambodian students,
available in Khmer and English, on any device, for free.

Learn — with 8 categories of official traffic rules —
and Play — a Game Center with multiple game modes.
Plus a live leaderboard, a reward system, and Suar AI,
our intelligent road safety assistant.
This is not a concept.
This is live at KesararamWithDigital.tech right now.

Technical Demo

Let me show you exactly how KWD works today.
A student opens the platform — it is already in their language.
They browse 8 content categories covering all national exam topics.

image infographics replace walls of text.

for students who learn better by listening.
Then they enter quiz mode —
our interactive questions test real understanding, not just memorization.
Their score appears instantly on our public leaderboard.
And the reward screen lets them exchange digital points
for real stationery from their teacher.
Every feature you see is live and fully functional.

Tech Stack & GenAI Usage
1:10 – 1:55
To scale this solution, we built like senior engineers —
using Next.js for our frontend,
Tailwind CSS for our design system,
and Neon DB as our serverless database.
Our true accelerator was Artificial Intelligence.
We used Claude AI for architecture decisions and code review.
We used Gemini for image prompt generation and content drafting.
We used ChatGPT for debugging and translation refinement.
And our systems were further trained using exclusive AI smart model data from our mentor.
One example prompt we used with Claude:
"You are a Cambodian road safety expert.
Explain this traffic rule to a 16-year-old student in simple Khmer and English."
That one prompt structure shaped our entire content tone.
And we are not stopping.
We are developing Suar AI —
an intelligent chat assistant to answer any road safety question in real-time.

C. Kanika
Technical Iteration
1:55 – 2:30
We did not build KWD in one step. We iterated.
Our first version was a paper sketch —
just a quiz flow drawn by hand.
Then we built a digital wireframe in Figma.
Then a basic HTML prototype.
Our mentor challenged us to go further —
so we rebuilt everything in Next.js for real performance.
After our first classroom test, students asked for Khmer audio.
We added voice reading.
After teacher feedback, we expanded from 5 content categories to 8.
After studying student motivation,
we added the physical reward redemption system.
Every version of KWD is better
because of real people telling us what was missing.

S. Runamara
Testing & Feedback Loops
2:30 – 3:10
We tested with real users from day one —
and every test made us better.
In classroom sessions at Kesararam Primary School,
we watched students use the platform live
and recorded everything that confused them.
We distributed feedback forms to students, parents, and teachers.
We interviewed our local police station
to verify the accuracy of our traffic rule content.
What did we hear?
Students needed Khmer language and audio support.
Parents wanted a reward to motivate their children at home.
Teachers asked for a way to track who was learning.
Police officers identified three content gaps in our original 5 categories.
And Claude AI itself suggested we redesign the quiz result display for clearer feedback.
We acted on all of it.
The platform you see today is shaped entirely by those voices.
